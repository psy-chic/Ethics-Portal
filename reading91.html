Guidance for Standards I–VII

STANDARD I: PROFESSIONALISM

The Standards of Professional Conduct comprise seven Standards (I–VII) and a total of 22 subsections. These Standards are detailed in the Standards of Practice Handbook, 11th Edition, 2014, published by CFA Institute.

Key Principle: Many Standards use terms open to interpretation such as "reasonable," "adequate," and "token." The exam will make clear whether actions violate a Standard rather than requiring you to draw fine distinctions about what is "reasonable" in a given situation.
Standard I(A) Knowledge of the Law
Members and Candidates must understand and comply with all applicable laws, rules, and regulations (including the CFA Institute Code of Ethics and Standards of Professional Conduct) of any government, regulatory organization, licensing agency, or professional association governing their professional activities. In the event of conflict, Members and Candidates must comply with the more strict law, rule, or regulation. Members and Candidates must not knowingly participate or assist in and must dissociate from any violation of such laws, rules, or regulations.

Key Points:

  • Don't violate any laws, rules, or regulations that apply to your professional activities, including the Code and Standards
  • Follow the most strict of: the Standards, country of residence laws, or local laws where doing business
  • If violations are occurring, dissociate from the activity or, in extreme cases, resign from the firm
  • CFA Institute strongly encourages members to report potential violations
Recommendations for Members:
  • Establish procedures to keep employees informed of changes in relevant laws, rules, and regulations
  • Review the firm's written compliance procedures regularly
  • Maintain copies of current laws, rules, and regulations
  • When in doubt about legality, consult supervisor, compliance personnel, or a lawyer
  • When dissociating from violations, keep records documenting the violations
  • Strongly encouraged to report violations to CFA Institute Professional Conduct Program
Recommendations for Firms:
  • Have a code of ethics
  • Provide employees with information on laws, rules, and regulations
  • Have procedures for reporting suspected violations
Standard I(B) Independence and Objectivity
Members and Candidates must use reasonable care and judgment to achieve and maintain independence and objectivity in their professional activities. Members and Candidates must not offer, solicit, or accept any gift, benefit, compensation, or consideration that reasonably could be expected to compromise their own or another's independence and objectivity.

Violations Include:

  • Allowing investment recommendations or analysis to be influenced by pressure or inducements
  • Allocating shares in oversubscribed IPOs to personal accounts
  • Accepting gifts that could compromise independence (gifts from clients are less problematic than from other parties)
  • Accepting compensation dependent on conclusions or market impact of reports
  • Failure to disclose that research is issuer-paid

Acceptable Activities:

  • Normal business entertainment is permitted
  • Preparing issuer-paid research if compensation is a flat rate not tied to conclusions and the fact is disclosed
  • Client gifts must be disclosed to employer prior to acceptance if possible, or after if not
Recommendations for Members:
  • Members or their firms should pay for their own travel to company events when practicable
  • Limit use of corporate aircraft to trips where commercial travel is not an alternative
Recommendations for Firms:
  • Restrict employee participation in IPOs and private placements; require pre-approval
  • Appoint a compliance officer with written policies on independence and objectivity
  • Have clear procedures for reporting violations
  • Limit gifts, other than from clients, to token items only
Standard I(C) Misrepresentation
Members and Candidates must not knowingly make any misrepresentations relating to investment analysis, recommendations, actions, or other professional activities.

Violations Include:

  • Presenting third-party research as your own without attribution (plagiarism)
  • Guaranteeing specific returns on securities without explicit guarantees
  • Selecting valuation services or benchmarks that mislead clients
  • Presenting performance data that omits accounts or relevant variables
  • Offering false or misleading information about capabilities, expertise, or experience
  • Using misleading marketing materials from third parties

Important Notes on Plagiarism:

  • Crediting the source is not required when using projections and statistics from recognized financial reporting services
  • When using work by other firm members, permitted to omit names of those no longer with firm, as long as not representing work as solely your own
Recommendations for Members:
  • Prepare a summary of experience, qualifications, and services able to perform
  • Encourage employers to develop procedures for verifying third-party marketing materials
  • Cite the source of any summaries of materials provided by others
  • Keep copies of all reports and materials used in research preparation
  • Provide a written list of firm's available services and qualifications
  • Periodically review documents for any misrepresentation
Standard I(D) Misconduct
Members and Candidates must not engage in any professional conduct involving dishonesty, fraud, or deceit or commit any act that reflects adversely on their professional reputation, integrity, or competence.

Key Points:

  • No dishonesty, fraud, or deceit in professional conduct
  • For non-professional conduct, the act must "reflect adversely on professional reputation, integrity, or competence"
  • Members must not use enforcement of this Standard to settle personal or political disputes unrelated to professional ethics
Recommendations for Firms:
  • Develop and adopt a code of ethics and make clear that unethical behavior will not be tolerated
  • Give employees a list of potential violations and sanctions, including dismissal
  • Check references of potential employees
STANDARD II: INTEGRITY OF CAPITAL MARKETS

This Standard addresses the integrity of capital markets through proper handling of material nonpublic information and prohibition of market manipulation.

Standard II(A) Material Nonpublic Information
Members and Candidates who possess material nonpublic information that could affect the value of an investment must not act or cause others to act on the information.

Key Definitions:

  • Material Information: Information that would affect the price of a security or that a reasonable investor would want before making an investment decision. Ambiguous information may not be material.
  • Nonpublic Information: Information that has not been made available to the marketplace. Analyst conference calls do not constitute public disclosure.

Important Points:

  • The prohibition extends to mutual funds containing the subject securities and related derivatives
  • Members may use material nonpublic information for its intended purpose in investment banking transactions, but not for other purposes unless it becomes public
  • Mosaic Theory: Reaching investment conclusions through perceptive analysis of public information combined with non-material nonpublic information is NOT a violation
Recommendations for Members:
  • Make reasonable efforts to achieve public dissemination of information possessed
  • Encourage firms to adopt procedures to prevent misuse of material nonpublic information
Recommendations for Firms:

Use a firewall within the firm, including:

  • Exercise substantial control of relevant interdepartmental communications through a clearance area (compliance or legal)
  • Review employee trades
  • Maintain "watch," "restricted," and "rumor" lists
  • Monitor and restrict proprietary trading while in possession of material nonpublic information
  • In some cases, only take the opposite side of unsolicited customer trades (prohibiting all proprietary trading may signal the market)
Standard II(B) Market Manipulation
Members and Candidates must not engage in practices that distort prices or artificially inflate trading volume with the intent to mislead market participants.

Key Points:

  • Member actions may affect security values and volumes without violating this Standard
  • The key is intent to mislead
  • Spreading false information to affect prices or volume is a violation
  • Making trades intended to mislead market participants is a violation
STANDARD III: DUTIES TO CLIENTS

This Standard establishes the duties owed to clients, including loyalty, fair dealing, suitability, performance presentation, and confidentiality.

Standard III(A) Loyalty, Prudence, and Care
Members and Candidates have a duty of loyalty to their clients and must act with reasonable care and exercise prudent judgment. Members and Candidates must act for the benefit of their clients and place their clients' interests before their employer's or their own interests.

Client interests always come first. Members must:

  • Exercise prudence, care, skill, and diligence under the circumstances
  • Manage pools of client assets in accordance with governing documents
  • Make investment decisions in the context of the total portfolio
  • Inform clients of any limitations in an advisory relationship
  • Vote proxies in an informed and responsible manner (cost-benefit may mean not voting all)
  • Use client brokerage (soft dollars/commissions) to benefit the client

Note: The "client" may be the investing public as a whole rather than a specific entity or person.

Recommendations for Members:
  • Submit to clients, at least quarterly, itemized statements showing all securities in custody and all debits, credits, and transactions
  • Encourage firms to address these topics in fiduciary duty policies:
    • Follow applicable rules and laws
    • Establish investment objectives of client
    • Consider suitability relative to client's needs and circumstances
    • Diversify
    • Deal fairly with all clients
    • Disclose conflicts
    • Disclose compensation arrangements
    • Vote proxies in best interest of clients
    • Maintain confidentiality
    • Seek best execution
Standard III(B) Fair Dealing
Members and Candidates must deal fairly and objectively with all clients when providing investment analysis, making investment recommendations, taking investment action, or engaging in other professional activities.

Key Points:

  • Do not discriminate against any clients when disseminating recommendations or taking investment action
  • "Fairly" does not mean "equally" - timing differences in communication receipt are normal
  • Different service levels are acceptable if disclosed to all clients and available to those willing to pay
  • Give all clients a fair opportunity to act on every recommendation
  • Clients unaware of a recommendation change should be advised before accepting orders
  • Do not take advantage of industry position to disadvantage clients (e.g., taking shares of oversubscribed IPOs)
Recommendations for Members:
  • Encourage firms to establish compliance procedures for proper dissemination and fair treatment
  • Maintain a list of clients and holdings to ensure fair treatment
Recommendations for Firms:
  • Limit people aware of recommendation changes before dissemination
  • Shorten time between decision and dissemination
  • Publish guidelines prohibiting pre-dissemination discussion or action
  • Disseminate new/changed recommendations simultaneously to interested clients
  • Develop written trade allocation procedures ensuring fairness, timely execution, and accuracy
  • Disclose trade allocation procedures
  • Establish systematic account review to ensure no preferred treatment
  • Disclose available service levels
Standard III(C) Suitability
1. When in an advisory relationship, Members must:
a. Make reasonable inquiry into client's investment experience, risk/return objectives, and financial constraints before making recommendations or taking action, and reassess regularly.
b. Determine investment is suitable to client's financial situation and consistent with written objectives, mandates, and constraints.
c. Judge suitability in context of client's total portfolio.

2. When managing to a specific mandate, strategy, or style, Members must make only recommendations or actions consistent with stated objectives and constraints.

Advisory Relationships:

  • Gather client information at beginning in an Investment Policy Statement (IPS)
  • Consider clients' needs, circumstances, and risk tolerance
  • Consider whether leverage is suitable for the client

Fund Management:

  • Select only investments consistent with stated mandate
Unsolicited Trade Requests

If a client requests a trade the manager knows is unsuitable:

  • Do not make the trade until discussing unsuitability with client based on IPS
  • If minimal effect on portfolio: May follow firm policy after discussion; client must acknowledge understanding
  • If material effect on portfolio: Update IPS to accept changed risk profile, OR follow firm policy (may allow trade in separate client-directed account), OR reconsider the relationship
Recommendations for Members:
  • Put needs, circumstances, and objectives into written IPS for each client
  • Consider: type of client, separate beneficiaries, investor objectives (return/risk), investor constraints (liquidity, cash flows, time, tax, regulatory/legal), and performance benchmarks
  • Review investor objectives and constraints periodically
Standard III(D) Performance Presentation
When communicating investment performance information, Members and Candidates must make reasonable efforts to ensure that it is fair, accurate, and complete.

Violations Include:

  • Misstating performance or misleading clients about investment performance
  • Misrepresenting past performance or reasonably expected performance
  • Stating or implying ability to achieve returns similar to past performance

Requirements:

  • For brief presentations, make detailed information available on request
  • Indicate that presentation offers only limited information
Recommendations for Members:
  • Encourage firms to adhere to Global Investment Performance Standards (GIPS)
  • Consider sophistication of the audience
  • Present performance of weighted composite of similar portfolios rather than single account
  • Include terminated accounts as part of historical performance with clear statement of when terminated
  • Include all appropriate disclosures (model results, gross/net of fees, etc.)
  • Maintain data and records used to calculate performance
Standard III(E) Preservation of Confidentiality
Members and Candidates must keep information about current, former, and prospective clients confidential unless:
1. The information concerns illegal activities on the part of the client;
2. Disclosure is required by law; or
3. The client or prospective client permits disclosure of the information.

Key Points:

  • If illegal activities by client are involved, members may have obligation to report to authorities
  • Confidentiality Standard extends to former clients
  • Requirements do not prevent cooperation with CFA Institute Professional Conduct Program (PCP) investigations
Recommendations for Members:
  • Avoid disclosing information except to authorized coworkers also working for the client
  • Follow firm procedures for storage of electronic data
  • Recommend adoption of such procedures if not in place
STANDARD IV: DUTIES TO EMPLOYERS

This Standard establishes the duties owed to employers, including loyalty, disclosure of compensation arrangements, and supervisory responsibilities.

Standard IV(A) Loyalty
In matters related to their employment, Members and Candidates must act for the benefit of their employer and not deprive their employer of the advantage of their skills and abilities, divulge confidential information, or otherwise cause harm to their employer.

Key Points:

  • This Standard applies to employees; independent contractors have duty to abide by agreement terms
  • Must not engage in activities that injure the firm, deprive it of profit, or deprive it of employees' skills
  • Always place client interests above employer interests, but consider effects on firm integrity and sustainability
  • No requirement to put employer interests ahead of family and personal obligations
  • Isolated cases may exist where duty to employer may be violated to protect clients or market integrity (not for personal gain)

Independent Practice:

  • For compensation is allowed if notification provided to employer fully describing all aspects (compensation, duration, nature of activities)
  • Employer must consent to all terms before practice begins

When Leaving an Employer:

  • Must continue to act in employer's best interests until resignation is effective
  • Violations include: Misappropriation of trade secrets, misuse of confidential information, soliciting employer's clients before leaving, self-dealing, misappropriation of client lists
  • Employer records on any medium (computer, tablet, phone) are property of the firm
  • Simple knowledge of former clients' names is generally not confidential
  • No prohibition on use of experience or knowledge gained at former employer
  • Must adhere to employer policies concerning social media
Recommendations for Members:
  • Give employer a copy of the Code and Standards
  • Best practice: use separate social media accounts for personal and professional communications
Recommendations for Firms:
  • Employers should not have incentive and compensation systems that encourage unethical behavior
Standard IV(B) Additional Compensation Arrangements
Members and Candidates must not accept gifts, benefits, compensation, or consideration that competes with or might reasonably be expected to create a conflict of interest with their employer's interest unless they obtain written consent from all parties involved.

Key Points:

  • Compensation includes direct and indirect compensation from client and benefits from third parties
  • Written consent from employer includes email communication

Difference Between Additional Compensation and Gift:

  • Additional Compensation Arrangement: Client offers bonus depending on future account performance - requires written consent in advance
  • Gift: Client offers bonus to reward past account performance - requires disclosure to employer (Standard I(B))
Recommendations for Members:
  • Make immediate written report to employer detailing any proposed compensation and services
  • Part-time workers should discuss competing arrangements at time of hire and abide by employer limitations
Recommendations for Firms:
  • Details of additional compensation, including performance incentives, should be verified by the offering party
Standard IV(C) Responsibilities of Supervisors
Members and Candidates must make reasonable efforts to ensure that anyone subject to their supervision or authority complies with applicable laws, rules, regulations, and the Code and Standards.

Key Points:

  • Must make reasonable efforts to prevent and detect violations
  • Adequate compliance system must meet industry standards, regulatory requirements, and Code/Standards requirements
  • Obligation to bring inadequate compliance system to management's attention and recommend corrective action
  • If no compliance procedures or inadequate procedures exist, must decline supervisory responsibility in writing until adequate procedures adopted
  • If violation occurs: respond promptly, conduct thorough investigation, increase supervision or place limitations on wrongdoer's activities
Recommendations for Members:

Once compliance program is instituted, supervisor should:

  • Distribute it to proper personnel
  • Update as needed
  • Continually educate staff regarding procedures
  • Issue reminders as necessary
  • Require professional conduct evaluations
  • Review employee actions to monitor compliance and identify violations
Recommendations for Firms:

Adequate compliance procedures should:

  • Be clearly written and easy to understand
  • Designate a compliance officer with clearly defined authority
  • Have a system of checks and balances
  • Outline the scope of procedures
  • Outline what conduct is permitted
  • Contain procedures for reporting violations and sanctions
  • Structure incentives so unethical behavior is not rewarded

Note: Do not commingle compliance procedures with code of ethics - this can dilute ethical obligations

STANDARD V: INVESTMENT ANALYSIS, RECOMMENDATIONS, AND ACTIONS

This Standard addresses the conduct of members in their investment analysis, recommendations, and actions.

Standard V(A) Diligence and Reasonable Basis
Members and Candidates must:
1. Exercise diligence, independence, and thoroughness in analyzing investments, making investment recommendations, and taking investment actions.
2. Have a reasonable and adequate basis, supported by appropriate research and investigation, for any investment analysis, recommendation, or action.

Key Points:

  • Application depends on investment philosophy, role in decision-making, and resources/support provided
  • Level of research needed depends on product or service offered

Things to Consider Before Making Recommendations:

  • Global and national economic conditions
  • Firm's financial results, operating history, and business cycle stage
  • Fees and historical results for mutual funds
  • Limitations of any quantitative models used
  • Whether peer group comparisons for valuation are appropriate
Recommendations for Members:

Criteria for judging quality of third-party research:

  • Review assumptions used
  • Determine how rigorous the analysis was
  • Identify how timely the research is
  • Evaluate objectivity and independence of recommendations
Recommendations for Firms:
  • Policy requiring research reports have a basis that can be substantiated as reasonable and adequate
  • Detailed written guidance for proper research and due diligence
  • Measurable criteria for judging research quality; base analyst compensation on such criteria
  • Written procedures for scenario testing of computer models (range of scenarios, accuracy over time, sensitivity analysis)
  • Policy for evaluating outside information providers
  • Standards for evaluating external advisers with review frequency
Standard V(B) Communication With Clients and Prospective Clients
Members and Candidates must:
1. Disclose basic format and general principles of investment processes and promptly disclose material changes.
2. Disclose significant limitations and risks associated with the investment process.
3. Use reasonable judgment in identifying important factors and include them in communications.
4. Distinguish between fact and opinion in presentations.

Key Points:

  • All means and types of communication are covered, not just research reports
  • Must distinguish between opinions and facts
  • Always include basic characteristics of securities in research reports
  • Expectations based on statistical modeling are not facts
  • Must explain investment decision-making process to clients and prospects

Specific Requirements:

  • For structured securities, allocation strategies, or nontraditional investments: communicate risk factors specific to such investments
  • Communicate potential gains and losses clearly in terms of total returns
  • Communicate significant changes in risk characteristics of investments or strategies
  • Update clients regularly about changes in investment process, including newly identified risks and limitations
  • When using quantitative models: explain limitations and assumptions to provide context for judging uncertainty

Important Limitations to Disclose:

  • Liquidity: Ability to exit investment readily without significant extra cost
  • Capacity: Investment vehicle's ability to absorb additional investment without reducing returns
Recommendations for Members:
  • Selection of relevant factors can be judgment call - maintain records indicating nature of research
  • Be able to supply additional information if requested by client or report users
Standard V(C) Record Retention
Members and Candidates must develop and maintain appropriate records to support their investment analyses, recommendations, actions, and other investment-related communications with clients and prospective clients.

Key Points:

  • Must maintain research records supporting reasons for conclusions and any investment actions
  • Records are property of the firm
  • All communications with clients through any medium (emails, text messages) are records that must be retained
  • Member who changes firms must re-create analysis documentation using publicly available information or information from the company - must not rely on memory or materials from previous firm
Recommendations for Members:
  • If no regulatory standards or firm policies exist, Standard recommends seven-year minimum holding period
Recommendations for Firms:
  • This recordkeeping requirement is generally the firm's responsibility
STANDARD VI: CONFLICTS OF INTEREST

This Standard addresses the disclosure and management of conflicts of interest.

Standard VI(A) Disclosure of Conflicts
Members and Candidates must make full and fair disclosure of all matters that could reasonably be expected to impair their independence and objectivity or interfere with respective duties to their clients, prospective clients, and employer. Members and Candidates must ensure that such disclosures are prominent, are delivered in plain language, and communicate the relevant information effectively.

Key Points:

  • Must fully disclose to clients, prospects, and employers all actual and potential conflicts
  • Disclosures must be clearly stated
  • Requirement allows clients and prospects to judge motives and potential biases

Common Conflicts Requiring Disclosure:

  • Broker-dealer market-making activities
  • Board service
  • Actual ownership of stocks in companies recommended or held by clients (most common)
  • Compensation/bonus structure that creates incentives for immediate gains over longer-term returns

Additional Requirements:

  • When acting in advisory capacity, must disclose compensation structure
  • Must update disclosures in case of significant change in compensation
  • Must give employers enough information to judge impact of conflict
  • Take reasonable steps to avoid conflicts and report promptly if they occur
Recommendations for Members:
  • Any special compensation arrangements, bonus programs, commissions, and incentives should be disclosed
Standard VI(B) Priority of Transactions
Investment transactions for clients and employers must have priority over investment transactions in which a Member or Candidate is the beneficial owner.

Key Points:

  • Client transactions take priority over personal transactions and over firm transactions
  • Personal transactions include situations where member is beneficial owner
  • Personal transactions may be undertaken only after clients and employer have had adequate opportunity to act
  • Family member accounts that are client accounts should be treated like any client account - not disadvantaged
  • Must not act on information about pending trades for personal gain
  • Overriding consideration: personal trades must not disadvantage any clients
  • When requested, must fully disclose firm's personal trading policies
Recommendations for Members:
  • Can avoid conflicts from IPOs by not participating in them
  • Should encourage firms to adopt listed firm procedures if not in place
Recommendations for Firms:

Basic procedures addressing personal investing conflicts should include:

  • Establish limitations on employee participation in equity IPOs
  • Establish restrictions on private placement participation with strict limits and proper supervision
  • Establish blackout/restricted periods for employees in investment decision-making - no front running
  • Establish reporting procedures: duplicate trade confirmations, disclosure of personal holdings and beneficial ownership, preclearance procedures
Standard VI(C) Referral Fees
Members and Candidates must disclose to their employer, clients, and prospective clients, as appropriate, any compensation, consideration, or benefit received from or paid to others for the recommendation of products or services.

Key Points:

  • Must inform employers, clients, and prospects of any benefit received for referrals
  • Allows them to evaluate full cost of service and any potential partiality
  • All types of consideration must be disclosed
Recommendations for Members:
  • Should encourage firms to adopt clear procedures regarding compensation for referrals
  • Should provide employers with updates at least quarterly
Recommendations for Firms:
  • Firms that do not prohibit referral fees should have clear procedures for approval
  • Have policies regarding nature and value of referral compensation received
STANDARD VII: RESPONSIBILITIES AS A CFA INSTITUTE MEMBER OR CFA CANDIDATE

This Standard addresses conduct as CFA Institute members and CFA candidates.

Standard VII(A) Conduct as Participants in CFA Institute Programs
Members and Candidates must not engage in any conduct that compromises the reputation or integrity of CFA Institute or the CFA designation or the integrity, validity, or security of CFA Institute programs.

Violations Include:

  • Cheating on the CFA exam or any exam
  • Revealing anything about broad or specific topics tested, content of questions, or formulas required/not required
  • Not following rules and policies of the CFA Program
  • Giving confidential information on CFA Program to candidates or public
  • Improperly using the designation to further personal and professional goals
  • Misrepresenting information on Professional Conduct Statement (PCS) or CFA Institute Professional Development Program
  • Violating any CFA exam policies (calculator, personal belongings, Candidate Pledge)

Important Notes:

  • Members and candidates are not precluded from expressing opinions about exam program or CFA Institute
  • Must not reveal confidential information about CFA Program
  • Members who volunteer in CFA Program may not solicit or reveal information about questions or grading process
Standard VII(B) Reference to CFA Institute, the CFA Designation, and the CFA Program
When referring to CFA Institute, CFA Institute membership, the CFA designation, or candidacy in the CFA Program, Members and Candidates must not misrepresent or exaggerate the meaning or implications of membership in CFA Institute, holding the CFA designation, or candidacy in the CFA Program.

Violations Include:

  • Making promotional promises or guarantees tied to CFA designation
  • Over-promising individual competence or investment results (higher performance, less risk, etc.)
  • Misrepresenting or exaggerating meaning of CFA designation

Membership Requirements:

  • Sign the PCS annually
  • Pay CFA Institute membership dues annually
  • Failure results in no longer being an active member

Proper Use:

  • There is no partial CFA designation
  • Acceptable to state candidate completed program in three years if factual
  • Claiming superior ability because of this is not permitted
Recommendations for Members:
  • Should ensure firms are aware of proper references to CFA designation or candidacy
  • Errors in these references are common
